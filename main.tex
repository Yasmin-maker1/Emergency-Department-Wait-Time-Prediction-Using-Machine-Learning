\begin{abstract}
Emergency department (ED) overcrowding is a global healthcare crisis associated with
increased patient mortality, reduced care quality, and significant operational
inefficiencies. Accurate prediction of patient wait times at the point of triage can
empower clinical staff to proactively allocate resources and improve patient
communication. This paper presents our systematic literature review (SLR) identifying
and synthesizing twelve peer-reviewed research articles (2022--2025) on machine
learning approaches for ED wait time and length-of-stay prediction, followed by our
data wrangling, exploratory data analysis (EDA), and machine learning pipeline on the
Hospital Emergency Room Visits dataset (Kaggle, CC0 license), structured to mirror the
MIMIC-IV-ED benchmark. Our dataset comprises 9,216 ED visits with 17 raw features
expanded to 26 engineered features after preprocessing. The target variable
\texttt{ed\_los\_minutes} has a mean of 189.8 minutes, a median of 153.7 minutes, and
a standard deviation of 141.7 minutes. ESI acuity level is the strongest predictor
($r = -0.38$), with mean LOS ranging from 315.7 minutes (ESI~1) to 91.4 minutes
(ESI~5). Our review reveals that gradient boosting ensemble methods (XGBoost, LightGBM,
Random Forest) consistently achieve the strongest predictive performance. Key gaps
identified include the near-universal use of binary classification rather than
continuous wait time regression, limited triage-level segmentation, incomplete
SHAP-based interpretability, and insufficient fairness analysis. Our project directly
addresses these gaps through regression-based ML with SHAP explainability,
triage-stratified analysis, and demographic fairness assessment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Background and Motivation}
Emergency departments serve as the primary point of entry for acute, unscheduled
healthcare across the globe. Rising patient volumes, resource constraints, and the
unpredictable nature of emergency presentations have made ED overcrowding a recognized
public health crisis. Prolonged wait times---defined as the interval between patient
arrival and physician evaluation---are directly associated with increased in-hospital
mortality, patient dissatisfaction, and a higher rate of patients leaving without being
seen (LWBS).

Despite the severity of this problem, most EDs continue to rely on manual triage
scoring systems such as the Emergency Severity Index (ESI) and the Manchester Triage
Scale (MTS) to prioritize care. While these systems effectively rank urgency, they do
not provide quantitative estimates of how long a patient will wait. This gap between
urgency classification and wait time transparency is precisely where machine learning
(ML) can make a meaningful clinical contribution.

\subsection{Problem Definition}
This work is guided by the following central research question: \textit{How can
supervised machine learning algorithms trained on routinely collected ED triage data
accurately predict patient wait times, and what methodological approaches produce the
most clinically interpretable and generalizable models?}

\subsection{Objectives and Novel Contributions}
The specific objectives of this project are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Identify and synthesize the most relevant ML research on ED wait time and
    length-of-stay prediction published between 2022 and 2025.
    \item Evaluate the methodological quality, datasets, and model types used across
    twelve studies.
    \item Identify recurring research gaps regarding interpretability, triage-level
    segmentation, continuous regression, and fairness.
    \item Apply regression-based gradient boosting and random forest models to the ED
    dataset, incorporating SHAP-based feature importance~\cite{shap}, and segment
    predictions by ESI triage level.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope}
This work covers twelve peer-reviewed articles from reputable sources, all published
between 2022 and 2025, and applies the derived insights to a machine learning pipeline.
Ten of the twelve articles were published in 2024 or 2025, reflecting the rapidly
accelerating pace of research in this domain.

\subsection{Porto (2024) --- ML \& NLP for ED Triage}
Porto et al.~\cite{porto2024} present a PRISMA 2020 systematic review analyzing how ML
and NLP algorithms improve triage accuracy. Models reviewed included decision trees,
SVMs, neural networks (LSTM, BERT), and NLP approaches. ML models consistently
outperformed traditional triage scoring scales, but the review identified a complete
absence of continuous wait time regression and limited explainability work.

\subsection{Multiple Authors (2025) --- Admission Prediction from Triage}
This PRISMA-compliant SLR~\cite{slr2025} evaluated ML models on ED triage data for
predicting inpatient admissions. Seven studies with rigorous methodology achieved
AUC-ROC of 0.81--0.93, with random forest and gradient boosting as top performers,
confirming that triage features (vitals, ESI level, chief complaint) are strong
predictors.

\subsection{Wang et al.\ (2025) --- XGBoost Fairness in ED Wait Times}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025fair} developed an interpretable XGBoost model for predicting
prolonged ED wait times ($\geq$30 min) in ESI-3 patients. XGBoost achieved
AUC $\approx$ 0.72, and SHAP identified triage ESI level and arrival hour as the top
predictors. Performance disparities were found across race/ethnicity and insurance type,
providing a direct methodological template and performance benchmark for our project.

\subsection{Xie et al.\ (2022) --- MIMIC-IV-ED Benchmark Suite}
Xie et al.~\cite{xie2022} created the first open-source benchmark dataset and pipeline
for ED prediction using MIMIC-IV-ED ($\sim$400,000 ED visits). Gradient boosting and
random forest outperformed all clinical scoring systems. This paper provides the
preprocessing pipeline and benchmark structure our project builds upon.

\subsection{Ricciardi et al.\ (2024) --- ML for ED Length of Stay}
Ricciardi et al.~\cite{ricciardi2024} evaluated ML classifiers for predicting prolonged
ED LOS ($\geq$4 hours) on 496,172 admissions from Italy. Random Forest achieved best
performance (AUC $\approx$ 0.80), validating that triage level, arrival time, and
demographics are sufficient predictors---exactly our feature set.

\subsection{Seo et al.\ (2024) --- Wait Time Prediction with NLP}
Seo et al.~\cite{seo2024} developed ML models integrating structured triage data with
NLP-processed chief complaint text on 271,143 ED patients. LightGBM achieved the best
performance and a real-time clinical dashboard prototype was developed, directly
modeling our target outcome (ED waiting time).

\subsection{Wang, Sambamoorthi et al.\ (2025) --- Interpretable ML for ED Wait Time}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025interp} compared five ML models for predicting prolonged ED
wait times in ESI-3 patients using SHAP values and partial dependency plots. XGBoost
and Random Forest achieved the best performance, directly confirming our chosen model
family and interpretability approach. We extend this work with continuous regression,
all 5 ESI levels, and fairness analysis.

\subsection{Sulaiman et al.\ (2025) --- ML + TE2Rules for ED LOS}
Sulaiman et al.~\cite{sulaiman2025} applied a novel explainability framework combining
ensemble ML with TE2Rules rule extraction on 10 minimal triage/demographic variables,
advancing the explainability agenda beyond standard SHAP and motivating rule-based
explanation as a future extension of our pipeline.

\subsection{Wu et al.\ (2025) --- ED LOS Continuous Regression}
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025} developed ML regression models for predicting ED LOS as a
continuous variable on 187,028 cases. LightGBM achieved the best performance with the
lowest MAE and RMSE. Current emergency waiting time was identified as the single top
predictor, directly supporting our core decision to treat ED LOS as a continuous
regression problem.

\subsection{Vural et al.\ (2025) --- AI Framework for ED Overcrowding}
Vural et al.~\cite{vural2025} evaluated 11 ML algorithms for predicting ED waiting room
occupancy at 6-hour and 24-hour resolutions. TSiTPlus achieved MAE of 4.19 and
R$^2$ of 0.56 for hourly prediction, extending ED prediction from individual patients
to system-level capacity management.

\subsection{Lett et al.\ (2025) --- Intersectional Fairness in ED Admissions}
Lett et al.~\cite{lett2025} compared intersectional versus marginal fairness debiasing
strategies in ED admission prediction using MIMIC-IV-ED. AUROC was 0.85 for both
baseline and intersectionally debiased models, with intersectional debiasing reducing
subgroup calibration error by 22.3\% versus 11.3\% for marginal debiasing, directly
motivating the fairness analysis dimension of our project.

\subsection{Sun et al.\ (2024) --- ED-Copilot: LLM + RL}
Sun et al.~\cite{sun2024} introduced ED-Copilot, combining BioGPT with a reinforcement
learning agent to recommend lab tests that minimize ED wait time. The system reduced
average laboratory wait time by approximately 50\% ($\sim$4 hours to $\sim$2 hours),
representing the most advanced AI paradigm in the review.

\section{Synthesis and Identified Research Gaps}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Overall Trends}
A clear trend across all twelve articles confirms that gradient boosting ensemble
methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest) consistently outperform both traditional
clinical scoring systems and simpler linear models. Growing emphasis on SHAP-based
interpretability, consolidation around public benchmark datasets, and increasing
attention to algorithmic fairness are the three other major trends observed.

\subsection{Identified Research Gaps}
The following gaps recur systematically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Continuous regression:} Ten of twelve studies use binary
    classification. Only Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025} and Seo et al.~\cite{seo2024} model
    wait time as a continuous variable.
    \item \textbf{Triage-level segmentation:} No study reports performance broken down
    by all five ESI triage levels.
    \item \textbf{SHAP interpretability:} Only four of twelve studies apply SHAP;
    eight use no interpretability tool.
    \item \textbf{Public dataset reproducibility:} Nine of twelve studies use
    proprietary single-center data.
    \item \textbf{Fairness analysis:} Only two of twelve studies formally assess
    algorithmic fairness.
\end{itemize}

\section{Research Questions}
% =============================================================

Based on our literature review, the following research questions guide our project:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Prediction Accuracy):} Can regression-based gradient boosting
    models predict ED LOS as a continuous variable (in minutes) with lower error than
    binary classification baselines?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Triage Segmentation):} Do model performance and key predictive
    features vary significantly across ESI triage severity levels (1--5)?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Interpretability):} Which features most strongly drive ED LOS
    predictions according to SHAP value analysis, and do these align with the
    literature?
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Fairness):} Does predictive performance differ significantly
    across demographic subgroups (race/ethnicity, gender, age group)?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Data Cleaning and Preprocessing}
The following wrangling steps were applied sequentially:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Missing value imputation:} Zero rows were dropped due to missing
    target values (\texttt{ed\_los\_minutes} was complete). Numeric vital signs were
    imputed using median imputation. Categorical fields were imputed with their mode:
    gender $\rightarrow$ ``F'', race $\rightarrow$ ``WHITE'',
    arrival\_transport $\rightarrow$ ``WALK IN'', disposition $\rightarrow$ ``HOME''.
    After imputation, remaining missing values = 0.

    \item \textbf{Outlier detection and treatment:} IQR analysis identified
    415 outliers (4.50\%) in \texttt{ed\_los\_minutes} (upper fence: 460.8 min) and a
    combined 628 outliers across vital signs (0.44\%--2.42\%). Z-score analysis
    ($|Z|>3$) confirmed 158 extreme LOS values (1.71\%). Clinical reference bounds
    were applied: heart rate [30, 250] bpm; SBP [60, 280] mmHg; DBP [30, 180] mmHg;
    temperature [93, 107]$^\circ$F; SpO$_2$ [50, 100]\%; respiratory rate [4, 50]
    br/min; pain score [0, 10]; LOS [5, 4320] min. No values fell outside these
    clinical bounds after imputation.

    \item \textbf{Label encoding:} Four categorical features were label-encoded:
    gender ($F=0$, $M=1$); race (ASIAN=0, BLACK/AFRICAN AMERICAN=1, HISPANIC=2,
    OTHER=3, WHITE=4); arrival\_transport (AMBULANCE=0, OTHER=1, REFERRED=2,
    WALK IN=3); disposition (ADMITTED=0, HOME=1, LEFT WITHOUT BEING SEEN=2,
    OBSERVATION=3, TRANSFER=4).

    \item \textbf{Feature engineering:} Five new features were derived:
    \textit{shock\_index} (HR/SBP; mean=0.66, std=0.19, range: 0.21--1.97),
    \textit{pulse\_pressure} (SBP$-$DBP; mean=50.1 mmHg), \textit{arrival\_shift}
    (Day: 07:00--14:59; Evening: 15:00--22:59; Night: 23:00--06:59),
    \textit{is\_weekend} (25.0\% of visits), and \textit{age\_group} (Pediatric
    $\leq$18; Young Adult 19--40; Middle Age 41--65; Elderly $>$65).

    \item \textbf{Normalization:} StandardScaler was applied to all 19 numeric model
    features (mean $\approx$ 0, std $\approx$ 1), consistent with Wu et
    al.~\cite{wu2025} and Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025fair}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Feature Correlation Analysis}
Pearson correlation analysis reveals that \textit{acuity} has the strongest association
with \texttt{ed\_los\_minutes} ($r = -0.38$), confirming that lower ESI level numbers
(higher severity) correspond to longer LOS. All other features show very weak linear
correlations ($|r| \leq 0.02$), including temperature ($r = -0.02$), shock index
($r = 0.01$), and heart rate ($r = 0.01$). This confirms that ED LOS is a multivariate
nonlinear problem where ensemble models capturing feature interactions are essential.
The strong correlation between SBP and DBP motivates the engineered
\textit{pulse\_pressure} feature to reduce multicollinearity.

\subsection{ED LOS by ESI Triage Level}
Table~\ref{tab:losacuity} shows that LOS varies substantially across ESI levels,
confirming that a single pooled model would obscure important subgroup dynamics.
ESI~Level~1 patients have the longest mean LOS (315.7 min) due to intensive workups,
while ESI Level~5 patients have the shortest (91.4 min). ESI Level~3 accounts for the
largest proportion of visits (4,184 of 9,216; 45.4\%), consistent with Wang et
al.~\cite{wang2025fair}. This directly supports our Milestone~3 plan for
triage-stratified modeling.

\subsection{Temporal Patterns}
Visit volume peaks during daytime hours (10:00--20:00). LOS is longer for early morning
arrivals (02:00--06:00) as these patients tend to be more seriously ill. Evening
arrivals coincide with the highest patient volumes, which may contribute to crowding.
Table~\ref{tab:losshift} shows LOS by arrival shift. Night shift arrivals have the
highest mean LOS (191.4 min) with a wider distribution, consistent with reduced
staffing and a higher proportion of critically ill patients presenting after hours.
These findings validate \texttt{arrival\_hour} and \texttt{arrival\_shift} as
important predictors for Milestone~3, consistent with Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025interp}.

\subsection{Arrival Mode and Disposition}
Walk-in patients form the majority of arrivals (5,714; 62.0\%), followed by ambulance
(2,045; 22.2\%), referred (933; 10.1\%), and other (524; 5.7\%). Ambulance arrivals
show a higher mean LOS (192.5 min) than walk-in patients (189.2 min), consistent with
their higher acuity. Walk-in patients' high volume with relatively shorter LOS
reflects the proportion of lower-acuity visits. Home discharge dominates disposition
(5,302; 57.5\%), followed by admitted (2,632; 28.6\%), observation (475; 5.2\%),
transfer (426; 4.6\%), and left-without-being-seen (381; 4.1\%).

\subsection{Demographic Patterns and Fairness Signals}
Table~\ref{tab:losrace} shows mean LOS by race/ethnicity. White patients have the
highest mean LOS (193.3 min) while Hispanic patients have the lowest (180.9 min), a
difference of 12.4 minutes. By gender, males have a slightly higher mean LOS (190.9
min) than females (188.8 min), a difference of 2.1 minutes.
Table~\ref{tab:losage} shows LOS by age group: Young Adults have the longest mean LOS
(193.5 min) while Elderly patients have the shortest (179.2 min). These preliminary
disparities motivate the formal fairness analysis planned for Milestone~3, building on
the methodology of Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025fair} and Lett et al.~\cite{lett2025}.

\section{Machine Learning Pipeline and Intermediate Results}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Feature Set}
The final model feature set comprises 19 standardized variables:
\texttt{age}, \texttt{heartrate}, \texttt{sbp}, \texttt{dbp}, \texttt{temperature},
\texttt{o2sat}, \texttt{resprate}, \texttt{pain\_score}, \texttt{acuity},
\texttt{shock\_index}, \texttt{pulse\_pressure}, \texttt{arrival\_hour},
\texttt{is\_weekend}, \texttt{was\_admitted}, \texttt{gender\_encoded},
\texttt{race\_encoded}, \texttt{arrival\_transport\_encoded},
\texttt{arrival\_shift\_encoded}, \texttt{age\_group\_encoded}.

\subsection{Models Selected}
Based on the literature's consistent findings, we evaluate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Baseline:} Linear Regression, Ridge Regression
    \item \textbf{Tree-based:} Random Forest Regressor
    \item \textbf{Gradient Boosting:} XGBoost Regressor, LightGBM Regressor
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Train/Test Split}
Models are evaluated on MAE, RMSE, and R$^2$ on a held-out test set, consistent with
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025}. An 80/20 chronological train/test split is applied to simulate
real deployment conditions.

\subsection{Expected Results and SHAP Analysis}
Based on the correlation analysis ($r = -0.38$ for acuity) and literature findings,
ESI acuity level is expected to be the dominant SHAP predictor, consistent with Wang et
al.~\cite{wang2025fair} and Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025interp}. Arrival hour,
shock index, and \texttt{was\_admitted} are expected to rank among the top features.
Gradient boosting models (XGBoost, LightGBM) are expected to outperform the linear
baselines given the weak linear correlations and strong nonlinear interactions in the
feature space. Full model results, SHAP summary plots, and patient-level force plots
will be delivered in Milestone~3.

% =============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =============================================================

The twelve reviewed articles confirm that gradient boosting ensemble methods are
well-suited to predicting ED wait times, and no study to date has combined continuous
wait time regression, ESI-level segmentation, SHAP interpretability, demographic
fairness analysis, and a publicly accessible dataset in a single coherent framework.
Our wrangling pipeline successfully processed 9,216 ED visits from 17 raw features to
26 engineered features with zero remaining missing values. EDA confirms that ESI
acuity level ($r = -0.38$ with LOS) is the dominant predictor, with mean LOS ranging
from 315.7 min (ESI~1) to 91.4 min (ESI~5). Preliminary demographic disparities---up
to 12.4 minutes across race/ethnicity groups---motivate the formal fairness analysis
in Milestone~3. Our project builds directly on the methodological foundations of Xie
et al.~\cite{xie2022}, Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025fair}, Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025},
Lett et al.~\cite{lett2025}, and Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025interp} to deliver a
reproducible, interpretable, and fair ED wait time prediction system.
